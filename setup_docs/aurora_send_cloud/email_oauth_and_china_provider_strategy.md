## 1. Bullet Points

### A. Core Email Integration (Gmail / Outlook via OAuth 2.0)

- **Gmail and Microsoft Outlook integration via OAuth 2.0** — users authorize the platform to send emails on their behalf during each session.
- **Per-session authorisation** — no standing email send permission is stored; each session requires explicit user approval at **Gate 3**.
- **Audit trail logging** — all sent emails are logged with **timestamp, recipient, subject, and message ID**.
- **Rate limiting enforced** — a maximum number of emails per hour per user is applied to prevent spam classification.
- **RFQ reply monitoring** — inbound replies are monitored via the same OAuth integration; the **Response Parsing Agent** reads inbound replies only from **approved supplier email threads**.

### B. China-Focused Provider Strategy

- **We are focusing on AuroraSendCloud** — the global product line of the Chinese provider SendCloud (by Sohu) — as the China email provider, because — per `chinese_mail_providers.md` and the validated implementation notes in `AuroraSendCloud_Documentation.md` — it is the only provider in the comparison that offers **all** of the following together:
  - Domain-level authentication (SPF, DKIM — "Automated Security" two-CNAME mode or "Classic" single-TXT mode —, MX, and DMARC) **without per-sender/mailbox verification**.
  - **Dynamic "From" addresses** via the Basic Send REST API (`POST {SENDCLOUD_API_BASE}/api/mail/send`, `multipart/form-data`) once the domain passes SPF+DKIM. `apiUser`/`apiKey` are sent as body fields rather than Basic Auth or a bearer header.
  - **Native inbound reply-tracking** — its **Inbound Route** feature relays *replies to messages this app already sent* to an external webhook (`POST /email_poc/webhooks/inbound`), with no need to split MX to a third-party parser. This is narrower than a generic inbound mailbox: a cold email sent directly to a dynamic address (never sent from this app first) is **not** forwarded, and — per AuroraSendCloud's own docs — attachments on replies are **not** relayed at all (text/metadata only). Because our addresses are fully dynamic per conversation, the route must use a **catch-all regex (`.*`)** rather than a fixed prefix.
  - **Multi-region infrastructure** — Singapore (default), US, and Hong Kong/CN, each with its own base URL and region-locked credentials; the Hong Kong/CN region is the one to activate for delivery optimized toward Chinese ISPs (QQ, 163/NetEase, etc.).
  - A direct self-service registration path — this project's own AuroraSendCloud account (used from India, sending `mail.jobsetu.online`) was provisioned and is documented in `AuroraSendCloud_Documentation.md` with no mention of the +86 Mainland-mobile signup gate described for the domestic `sendcloud.net` product elsewhere in this document. **⚠️ Unconfirmed:** whether that gate still applies to new AuroraSendCloud signups generally — treat the +86/sandbox constraints in `chinese_mail_providers.md` as describing the domestic product until re-verified against AuroraSendCloud's current signup flow.
- **We are not using other transactional providers such as SendGrid** for Chinese delivery. Per the document, providers outside this China-specific list are only referenced as **inbound-MX-split workarounds** (e.g., "route inbound MX to Mailgun/SendGrid/Postmark") for providers that lack native inbound parse — they are not built for, or optimized around, Mainland China's delivery rules. Routing production mail for Chinese recipients through a non-China-optimized provider like SendGrid carries a higher risk of **delivery failure**, given the document's repeated notes that Mainland-bound mail is subject to:
  - **ICP Filing (备案) with MIIT** requirements for domains sending on domestic lines (flagged as a "Universal compliance requirement" in the document's takeaways).
  - **Keyword/content and anti-spam screening** on Mainland-outbound traffic.
  - The **Great Firewall** itself, which the document states purpose-built providers (MXtoChina, Tencent Cloud SES, AuroraSendCloud) are specifically engineered to route around — a capability generic non-China SMTP/API providers are not documented to have.

### C. Planned AuroraSendCloud Implementation Workflow

For RFQ emails routed to Chinese suppliers, we will use AuroraSendCloud end-to-end as follows:

1. **Subdomain verification** — verify our application's (IMS) own subdomain, e.g. **`mail.ims.com`**, with AuroraSendCloud by configuring the required DNS records: **SPF (TXT), DKIM (two CNAMEs under "Automated Security" mode, or a single TXT under "Classic" mode), MX, and DMARC (TXT, optional but recommended)**. This is **domain-level authentication** — per `AuroraSendCloud_Documentation.md`, once the subdomain is verified, no separate per-mailbox/per-sender verification is required. Pick the region (Singapore/US/Hong Kong) closest to where our servers run, since `apiUser`/`apiKey` credentials are region-locked and must be regenerated if the region ever changes.
2. **Dynamic "From" address generation** — once `mail.ims.com` is verified, we will generate a **dynamic "From" address per user/thread** (e.g. `james-000@mail.ims.com`) via AuroraSendCloud's Basic Send API (`POST {SENDCLOUD_API_BASE}/api/mail/send`, `multipart/form-data`, with `apiUser`/`apiKey` passed as body fields). AuroraSendCloud supports dynamic "From" addresses at the domain level with **no per-prefix registration**, so any thread-specific address under the verified subdomain can send immediately.
3. **Sending to the Chinese supplier** — the RFQ mail is sent to the Chinese supplier using this dynamically generated, thread-specific "From" address, on the user's behalf.
4. **Reply tracking via webhook** — the supplier's reply is captured through AuroraSendCloud's **Inbound Route** feature (Webhook URL method), which relays a reply to a message we already sent as a POST to our **external webhook URL** — no MX split to a third-party parser (e.g. Mailgun/SendGrid/Postmark) is needed, unlike every other provider in the comparison. Two caveats worth planning around: (a) the route must be configured with a **catch-all regex (`.*`)** since every address is a distinct dynamic prefix, not a fixed one, and takes up to **10 minutes** to activate after creation/edits; and (b) **attachments on replies are not relayed** by Inbound Route at all (confirmed in AuroraSendCloud's own docs — text/metadata only), so attachment capture on supplier replies would require pointing MX directly at our own mail server instead.
5. **No global/generic providers (e.g., SendGrid)** — reaffirming Section B: we are not using non-China-optimized providers like SendGrid for this flow, because Mainland-bound mail is subject to **ICP filing requirements, content/anti-spam screening, and the Great Firewall** — all of which raise the risk of **email delivery failure** for providers that are not purpose-built for Chinese ISPs.

## 3. Why AuroraSendCloud Over the Alternatives (Summary)

| Requirement (from our architecture) | AuroraSendCloud | Others (per file) |
|---|---|---|
| Dynamic "From" addresses (needed for per-supplier/per-thread sending) | ✅ Yes — domain-level, no per-prefix registration | Only MXtoChina and Tencent Cloud SES also support this; all mailbox-style products (NetEase, Alibaba Enterprise Mail, M365-21Vianet) do not |
| Native inbound reply parsing (needed for the Response Parsing Agent) | ⚠️ Yes, but narrower than assumed — Inbound Route only relays replies to a message we already sent (not a generic catcher), needs a catch-all regex for our dynamic addresses, and never relays attachments; only provider in the comparison with any built-in inbound webhook | ❌ No other provider in the document has native inbound parse; all require an MX-split workaround to a third party or self-hosted listener |

| Reason to avoid SendGrid-style generic providers | Not purpose-built for Mainland delivery; document lists such providers only as inbound-MX-split fallbacks, not as primary China-delivery options — combined with ICP filing rules, content screening, and the Great Firewall, this raises the risk of delivery failure | — |
